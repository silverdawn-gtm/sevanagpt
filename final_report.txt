                                 ABSTRACT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SevanaGPT is an AI-powered web application designed to bridge the information
gap between Indian citizens and the numerous government welfare schemes available
to them. India operates over 4,600 central and state-level welfare schemes across
categories such as agriculture, education, health, housing, and social welfare.
However, citizens often remain unaware of the schemes they are eligible for due
to fragmented information, language barriers, and complex eligibility criteria.

SevanaGPT addresses this problem by providing a conversational AI chatbot, a
hybrid search engine, and an eligibility-checking wizard that together make
scheme discovery intuitive and accessible. The platform supports 12 Indian
languages including Hindi, Tamil, Telugu, Bengali, Marathi, Gujarati, Kannada,
Malayalam, Punjabi, Odia, and Urdu, ensuring inclusivity for a diverse user base.

The system is built on a modern technology stack comprising a Next.js React
frontend, a FastAPI Python backend, PostgreSQL with pgvector for vector-based
semantic search, and integrations with Mistral AI for natural language
understanding, Bhashini for voice interaction (ASR/TTS), and IndicTrans2 for
neural machine translation. The dataset of 4,600+ schemes is aggregated from
multiple authoritative sources including MyScheme.gov.in, curated Hugging Face
datasets, and state-specific repositories.

Key contributions of this project include: (1) a finite state machine (FSM)
based conversational agent that guides users through scheme discovery, (2) a
hybrid search pipeline combining keyword, semantic, and translation-aware search
using Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF), (3) real-time multilingual support with
caching and fallback mechanisms, and (4) voice-based interaction for users with
limited literacy. The system is containerized using Docker Compose for
reproducible deployment and is designed with scalability, modularity, and
accessibility as core principles.

1.1  Background and Motivation
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The Government of India, along with various state governments, operates
thousands of welfare schemes aimed at uplifting citizens across socioeconomic
categories. These schemes cover a wide spectrum of domains including agriculture,
education, healthcare, housing, employment, social security, and women and child
development. As of 2024, the central government alone administers over 740
schemes, while individual states add hundreds more, bringing the cumulative total
to well over 4,600 documented schemes.

Despite this extensive network of support, a significant portion of the target
population remains unaware of the schemes available to them. The reasons for this
disconnect are multifaceted:

  - Fragmented Information: Scheme details are scattered across multiple
    government portals, ministry websites, and state-level platforms with no
    unified discovery interface.

  - Language Barriers: Most scheme documentation is available primarily in
    English or Hindi, excluding a large section of the population that
    communicates in regional languages.

  - Complex Eligibility Criteria: Schemes often have intricate eligibility
    requirements based on age, gender, income, caste, occupation, disability
    status, and geographic location. Citizens struggle to determine which
    schemes apply to them without expert guidance.

  - Digital Literacy Gap: Rural and semi-urban populations, who are often the
    primary beneficiaries of welfare schemes, may lack the digital literacy
    required to navigate complex web portals.

These challenges create a critical need for an intelligent, multilingual, and
accessible platform that can guide citizens toward the right schemes based on
their individual profiles and needs.


1.2  Problem Statement
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The core problem addressed by SevanaGPT is: "How can technology make government
welfare scheme discovery accessible, personalized, and inclusive for every Indian
citizen, regardless of their language, literacy level, or technical proficiency?"

Specifically, the project tackles:

  1. Information Overload: With 4,600+ schemes, users cannot manually browse
     and evaluate every option. An intelligent search and recommendation system
     is needed.

  2. Language Exclusion: A system that operates only in English or Hindi
     inherently excludes hundreds of millions of speakers of other Indian
     languages.

  3. Eligibility Confusion: Citizens need a guided mechanism to input their
     demographic details and receive personalized scheme matches.

  4. Accessibility: Voice-based interaction is essential for users who may not
     be comfortable with text-based interfaces.


1.3  Objectives
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The primary objectives of the SevanaGPT project are:

  1. To develop an AI-powered conversational chatbot that understands user
     needs in natural language and recommends relevant government schemes.

  2. To implement a hybrid search engine that combines keyword matching,
     semantic understanding, and translation-aware retrieval for accurate
     scheme discovery.

  3. To build an eligibility-checking wizard that matches user profiles
     against scheme criteria and returns personalized recommendations.

  4. To provide multilingual support for 12 Indian languages with real-time
     translation capabilities.

  5. To integrate voice interaction (speech-to-text and text-to-speech)
     for inclusive access.

  6. To aggregate scheme data from multiple authoritative sources into a
     comprehensive and up-to-date knowledge base.

  7. To design a scalable, containerized system architecture suitable for
     production deployment.


1.4  Scope of the Project
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SevanaGPT covers the following functional scope:

  - Scheme browsing by category, state, ministry, and tags
  - Free-text and voice-based scheme search
  - AI chatbot with contextual conversation management
  - Eligibility checking based on user demographics
  - Multilingual interface and content translation
  - Featured scheme highlights and recommendations

The project does not include:

  - Actual scheme application or form submission
  - Integration with Aadhaar or DigiLocker for identity verification
  - Real-time scheme status tracking or benefit disbursement
  - Mobile native application (only responsive web)

This chapter provides a comprehensive description of the system architecture and

The system follows a microservices-oriented architecture with four primary
components orchestrated via Docker Compose:

  1. Frontend  - Next.js React web application (Port 3000)
  2. Backend   - FastAPI Python API server (Port 8000)

  filter by category, state, level, and keyword. Pagination parameters are
  managed via URL query strings for shareable links.

  The detail page renders comprehensive information about a single scheme
  including its full description, benefits, eligibility criteria, application
  process, documents required, official links, and associated FAQs. Content
  is dynamically translated based on the user's selected language.

Category Browsing (/categories and /categories/[slug]):
  The categories page displays a grid of 15+ scheme categories (Agriculture,
  Education, Health, Housing, etc.) with icons and scheme counts. Clicking a
  category navigates to a filtered list of schemes within that category.

State Browsing (/states and /states/[slug]):
  Provides browsing by Indian states and union territories. State-specific
  pages include filters for scheme level (central, state, or both) and display
  the relevant ministry/department information.

Ministry Browsing (/ministries and /ministries/[slug]):
  Lists government ministries and departments with their associated schemes.
  Supports both central and state-level ministry browsing.

Find Scheme (/find-scheme):
  This page hosts the Eligibility Wizard, a multi-step questionnaire that
  collects user demographics (age, gender, state, income, caste, occupation,
  disability status, student status, BPL status) and matches them against
  scheme eligibility criteria to return personalized recommendations.

Chat (/chat):
  The full-page chat interface provides a conversational experience with the
  AI chatbot. Features include message history, scheme card rendering within
  chat messages, quick-reply suggestion chips, and voice input/output
  capabilities via the Bhashini ASR/TTS integration.


2.1.4  Key Components

ChatWidget and ChatWindow:
  The ChatWidget is a floating action button (FAB) that appears on every page,
  providing instant access to the AI chatbot without navigating away. When
  activated, it opens the ChatWindow component which renders the conversation
  history, manages the message input area, and displays scheme recommendations
  as interactive cards within the chat flow.

EligibilityWizard:
  This component implements a step-by-step form with progressive disclosure.
  Each step collects a specific demographic attribute. The wizard maintains
  internal state and submits the complete profile to the eligibility API
  endpoint. Results are displayed as a ranked list of matching schemes with
  relevance indicators.

LanguageSwitcher and Translation (T):
  The LanguageSwitcher component renders a dropdown menu of 12 supported
  languages. Language selection is managed globally via the LanguageContext,
  which loads the appropriate translation JSON file and provides a translation
  function to all child components. The T component is a convenience wrapper
  that renders translated strings inline.

SchemeCard:
  A reusable card component that displays scheme metadata including name,
  description snippet, category badge, level indicator (central/state), and
  action buttons. It is used across the browse pages, search results, chat
  messages, and eligibility wizard results.


2.1.5  State Management and Hooks

LanguageContext:
  A React Context provider that manages the application's current language
  state. It stores the selected language code (e.g., "en", "hi", "ta"),
  loads the corresponding translation dictionary from JSON files in the
  messages/ directory, and exposes a translate function that accepts a key
  and returns the localized string. The context persists language selection
  across page navigations.

useChat Hook:
  This custom hook encapsulates all chat-related logic including:
    - Session ID generation and management
    - Message history state (array of user and assistant messages)
    - API communication with the chat endpoint
    - Scheme card extraction from chat responses
    - Suggestion/quick-reply management
    - Voice response handling (receiving TTS audio URLs)

useVoiceRecorder Hook:
  Manages the browser's MediaRecorder API for capturing audio input. It
  handles microphone permission requests, audio recording start/stop,
  audio blob creation, and submission to the voice transcription endpoint.
  The transcribed text is then fed into the chat pipeline.

2.2.4  Chatbot Implementation

The chatbot is the core interactive component of SevanaGPT. It uses a Finite
State Machine (FSM) architecture to manage conversation flow:

  States:
    GREETING         - Initial state; welcome message and topic suggestions
    NEED_EXTRACTION  - Clarifying user intent (category, location, demographics)
    SCHEME_SEARCH    - Performing search and presenting matched schemes
    DISAMBIGUATION   - Narrowing down results when too many matches found
    SCHEME_DETAIL    - Providing detailed information about a specific scheme
    CLOSING          - Concluding the conversation

  Transitions:
    GREETING -> NEED_EXTRACTION     (on: search_scheme intent)
    GREETING -> SCHEME_DETAIL       (on: ask_detail intent)
    NEED_EXTRACTION -> SCHEME_SEARCH (on: sufficient context gathered)
    SCHEME_SEARCH -> SCHEME_DETAIL  (on: user selects a scheme)
    SCHEME_SEARCH -> DISAMBIGUATION (on: too many results)
    ANY STATE -> CLOSING            (on: goodbye intent)

Intent Classification:
  User messages are classified into intents using Mistral AI with a structured
  prompt. The supported intents are: search_scheme, ask_detail,
  check_eligibility, clarify, goodbye, greeting, and other. A keyword-based
  fallback mechanism handles cases where the LLM is unavailable.

Chat Pipeline:
  1. Receive user message (text or transcribed voice)
  2. Classify intent using LLM or keyword fallback
  3. Determine FSM state transition based on current state and intent
  4. Extract entities from conversation context (age, gender, state, category)
  5. If in SCHEME_SEARCH state, perform hybrid search
  6. Generate contextual response using LLM with scheme data as context
  7. Translate response to user's preferred language if not English
  8. Return response with scheme cards and suggestion chips
  9. Persist conversation state and messages to database


2.2.5  Hybrid Search Engine

The search system implements a multi-strategy approach combined using
Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF):

  Strategy 1 - Keyword Search:
    Performs PostgreSQL ILIKE pattern matching on scheme name, description,
    and benefits fields. Handles partial matches and case-insensitive queries.

  Strategy 2 - Semantic Search:
    Converts the user query to a 1024-dimensional embedding using Mistral
    Embed, then performs cosine similarity search against pre-computed scheme
    embeddings stored in the pgvector column. This captures meaning-based
    matches even when exact keywords are absent.

  Strategy 3 - Translation-Aware Search:
    Searches the scheme_translations table which contains pre-translated
    scheme content in multiple languages. This enables users searching in
    Hindi or other languages to find schemes whose original content is in
    English.

  Fusion - Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF):
    Results from all three strategies are merged using the RRF formula:

      score(scheme) = SUM over lists [ 1 / (k + rank_in_list + 1) ]

    where k = 60 (smoothing constant). Schemes appearing in multiple result
    lists receive boosted scores, promoting high-confidence matches.


2.2.6  Translation Services

SevanaGPT employs a multi-tier translation strategy:

  Tier 1 - Static Dictionaries:
    Pre-computed translations for fixed UI content such as category names,
    state names, ministry names, and common labels in all 12 languages.
    These are stored in Python dictionaries for instant lookup.

  Tier 2 - Pre-computed Scheme Translations:
    The scheme_translations database table stores translated versions of
    scheme names, descriptions, benefits, eligibility criteria, and other
    fields. These are generated in batch using the pre_translate.py script.

  Tier 3 - Bhashini NMT (Neural Machine Translation):
    For dynamic content (chat responses, search results), the Bhashini API
    provides real-time translation. This is the primary translation service
    for the voice pipeline.

  Tier 4 - IndicTrans2 Microservice:
    An optional GPU-accelerated translation service running the AI4Bharat
    IndicTrans2 model (200M parameters). Provides high-quality English-to-
    Indic language translation with batch processing support.

  Tier 5 - Google Translate Fallback:
    When Bhashini and IndicTrans are unavailable, the deep-translator
    library provides Google Translate as a final fallback. Results are
    cached in the translation_cache database table to minimize API calls.


2.2.7  Voice Interaction

Voice interaction is powered by the Bhashini platform:

  Speech-to-Text (ASR):
    - Accepts audio input from the browser's MediaRecorder API
    - Sends audio to Bhashini ASR with the appropriate language model
    - Returns transcribed text which is then processed by the chat pipeline
    - Supports all 12 languages

  Text-to-Speech (TTS):
    - Converts the chatbot's text response to speech audio
    - Uses language-specific Bhashini TTS models
    - Returns audio data that is played back in the browser
    - Enables fully voice-based interaction for users with limited literacy

  Rate Limiting:
    Voice endpoints are rate-limited more strictly (10 requests per 60 seconds
    per IP) compared to text chat (30 requests per 60 seconds per IP) due to
    higher computational cost.


2.2.8  Middleware and Utilities

Request Timing Middleware:
  Tracks response times for all API endpoints and exposes aggregate metrics
  (average, P95, P99 latencies) via the /metrics endpoint for performance
  monitoring.

Rate Limiting:
  Implements a token-bucket algorithm for per-IP rate limiting on chat and
  voice endpoints. Returns HTTP 429 (Too Many Requests) when limits are
  exceeded.

Slug Generation:
  Creates URL-friendly identifiers from scheme, category, state, and ministry
  names. Used throughout the application for clean, shareable URLs (e.g.,
  /schemes/pm-kisan, /states/maharashtra).

2.3  Database Implementation
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

2.3.1  Database Selection

PostgreSQL 16 was selected as the database engine for the following reasons:

  - Robust relational data modeling for structured scheme metadata
  - pgvector extension for native vector similarity search (semantic search)
  - JSONB column support for flexible, schema-less data (chat context, extras)
  - Full-text search capabilities for keyword matching
  - Mature async driver support via asyncpg
  - Strong indexing and query optimization features
  - Production-proven reliability and scalability

The pgvector extension is critical as it enables storing and querying 1024-
dimensional embedding vectors directly in the database, eliminating the need
for a separate vector database like Pinecone or Weaviate.


2.3.2  Database Schema

The database consists of the following tables:

Core Tables:

  schemes
  ├── id                    : Integer, Primary Key, Auto-increment

  ├── eligibility_criteria  : Text

  ├── ministry_id           : Integer, Foreign Key -> ministries.id
  ├── level                 : String (central/state)
  ├── target_gender         : ARRAY(String)
  ├── min_age               : Integer
  ├── max_age               : Integer
  ├── target_social_category: ARRAY(String)
  ├── target_income_max     : Integer

2.3.3  Entity Relationships

The database schema enforces the following relationships:

  - One-to-Many: Category -> Schemes (a category contains many schemes)
  - One-to-Many: Ministry -> Schemes (a ministry manages many schemes)
  - Many-to-Many: Schemes <-> States (schemes can be available in multiple
    states, and states have multiple schemes; via scheme_states join table)
  - Many-to-Many: Schemes <-> Tags (schemes can have multiple tags, and tags
    can apply to many schemes; via scheme_tags join table)
  - One-to-Many: Scheme -> SchemeEmbeddings (one embedding per scheme)
  - One-to-Many: Scheme -> SchemeTranslations (one translation per language)
  - One-to-Many: Scheme -> SchemeFAQs (multiple FAQ pairs per scheme)
  - One-to-Many: Conversation -> Messages (a conversation has many messages)
  - One-to-One: Ministry -> State (state-level ministries link to their state)

3.1  Data Sources
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SevanaGPT aggregates government scheme data from five distinct sources to build
a comprehensive knowledge base of 4,600+ schemes:

  Source 1 - Curated Hugging Face Dataset (~100 schemes):
    A manually curated dataset of major central and state government schemes
    hosted on Hugging Face. This dataset provides high-quality, well-structured
    entries with complete metadata including eligibility criteria, benefits,
    application process, and documents required. It covers schemes from the
    central government and four major states. Ingested via ingest_hf.py.

  Source 2 - State-Specific Schemes (~113 schemes):
    A custom-compiled dataset covering schemes from 14 Indian states that
    are not well-represented in other sources. These schemes are defined
    directly in the ingestion script with complete metadata and are mapped
    to appropriate categories, states, and ministries. Ingested via
    ingest_state_schemes.py.

  Source 3 - MyScheme.gov.in API (~4,530 schemes):
    The largest data source, accessing the official Government of India
    MyScheme portal API. The ingestion script fetches schemes in batches
    of 50, extracting scheme names, descriptions, benefits, eligibility
    criteria, categories, and state/ministry associations. The script handles
    category name normalization (e.g., comma variations), state name mapping,
    and deduplication. Ingested via ingest_myscheme.py.

  Source 4 - Kaggle Datasets (Variable):
    Optional integration with Kaggle datasets related to Indian government
    schemes. Requires Kaggle API credentials. Provides supplementary data
    that may include historical or analytical scheme information. Ingested
    via ingest_kaggle.py.

  Source 5 - data.gov.in (Variable):
    Optional integration with India's Open Government Data platform. Requires
    a data.gov.in API key. Can provide official scheme data from the national
    open data repository. Ingested via ingest_datagov.py.


3.2  Data Ingestion Pipeline
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The data ingestion follows a sequential pipeline managed through Makefile
commands:

  Step 1 - Reference Data Seeding (seed.py):
    Populates the database with foundational reference data:
    - 15+ Categories with icons and display order
      (Agriculture, Education, Health, Housing, Employment, Women & Child,
       Social Welfare, Banking & Insurance, Science & Technology, etc.)
    - 36 States and Union Territories with ISO codes and UT flags
    - 50+ Ministries at both central and state levels
    - Tags for scheme classification (Cash Transfer, Subsidy, Loan,
      Scholarship, Training, Infrastructure, etc.)

  Step 2 - Curated Schemes (ingest_hf.py):
    Downloads and processes the Hugging Face dataset. Each scheme is:
    - Parsed for structured fields (name, description, benefits, etc.)
    - Mapped to existing categories using fuzzy matching
    - Associated with relevant states via the scheme_states join table
    - Assigned appropriate ministry links
    - Tagged with relevant scheme type tags
    - Saved with source='hf' for provenance tracking

  Step 3 - State Schemes (ingest_state_schemes.py):
    Processes the state-specific scheme collection covering 14 states
    including Meghalaya, Mizoram, Nagaland, Manipur, Tripura, Sikkim,
    Arunachal Pradesh, Goa, Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Uttarakhand, Himachal
    Pradesh, Haryana, and Punjab. Each scheme includes complete metadata
    and is mapped to the appropriate state and state-level ministry.

  Step 4 - MyScheme.gov.in (ingest_myscheme.py):
    The largest ingestion step, which:
    - Calls the MyScheme API at https://api.myscheme.gov.in/search/v6/schemes
    - Fetches schemes in paginated batches of 50
    - Extracts and normalizes category names
    - Maps state names (handling variations like "dadra & nagar haveli"
      vs "Dadra and Nagar Haveli and Daman and Diu")
    - Creates new ministry entries when encountered
    - Performs deduplication based on scheme slug
    - Marks source='myscheme' for provenance
    - Is idempotent (safe to re-run without creating duplicates)

  Step 5 - Embedding Generation (generate_embeddings.py):
    After all scheme data is ingested, vector embeddings are generated:
    - For each scheme, a text representation is composed:
      "{scheme_name} {description} {benefits}"
    - This text is sent to the Mistral Embed API to produce a
      1024-dimensional vector
    - The vector is stored in the scheme_embeddings table using pgvector
    - A text hash is stored to detect when embeddings need regeneration
    - Existing embeddings are skipped (incremental processing)

  Step 6 - Pre-Translation (pre_translate.py):
    Optionally pre-computes translations for scheme content:
    - Translates scheme names and descriptions into Hindi (and optionally
      other languages)
    - Stores results in the scheme_translations table
    - Reduces runtime translation latency for frequently accessed content
    - Additional languages are translated on-demand and cached


3.3  Data Quality and Normalization
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Several data quality measures are implemented across the ingestion pipeline:

  Category Normalization:
    Category names from different sources may use inconsistent formatting
    (e.g., "Agriculture, Rural & Environment" vs "Agriculture,Rural &
    Environment"). The ingestion scripts normalize category names by
    standardizing comma spacing and matching against the seeded categories.

  State Name Mapping:
    State names vary across sources. A mapping dictionary handles variations
    such as "Andaman & Nicobar Islands" vs "Andaman and Nicobar Islands",
    "Dadra & Nagar Haveli" vs "Dadra and Nagar Haveli and Daman and Diu".

  Deduplication:
    Scheme slugs serve as unique identifiers. Before inserting a new scheme,
    the ingestion scripts check for existing schemes with the same slug.
    If found, the existing record is updated rather than duplicated.

  Source Tracking:
    Each scheme record includes a 'source' field indicating its data origin
    (hf, myscheme, kaggle, datagov, state_specific). This enables data
    quality analysis and source-specific updates.

  Missing Data Handling:
    Schemes with incomplete metadata are still ingested but with NULL values
    for missing fields. The frontend gracefully handles missing data by
    showing "Information not available" placeholders.

                    CHAPTER 4: SYSTEM TESTING

4.1  Testing Framework and Setup
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The project employs pytest as the primary testing framework with the following
extensions:

  - pytest-asyncio (0.24.0): Enables testing of async/await functions and
    async database operations.
  - pytest-benchmark (5.1.0): Provides benchmarking capabilities for
    performance testing and regression detection.
  - sacrebleu (2.4.3): Implements BLEU score evaluation for measuring
    translation quality against reference translations.

Test configuration is centralized in conftest.py, which provides shared
fixtures including mock database sessions, sample scheme data, and test
configuration overrides.


4.2  Test Categories and Coverage
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

4.2.1  Chatbot FSM Tests (test_chat_service.py)

These tests validate the Finite State Machine that governs conversation flow:

  - State transition correctness: Verifies that each (state, intent) pair
    produces the expected next state. For example:
      GREETING + search_scheme -> NEED_EXTRACTION
      NEED_EXTRACTION + clarify -> NEED_EXTRACTION (stays in same state)
      SCHEME_SEARCH + ask_detail -> SCHEME_DETAIL
      ANY + goodbye -> CLOSING

  - Edge cases: Tests invalid state-intent combinations to ensure graceful
    handling without crashes.

  - Context persistence: Validates that extracted entities (age, gender,
    state) are correctly maintained across state transitions.


4.2.2  Eligibility Matching Tests (test_eligibility.py)

These tests verify the eligibility-checking algorithm:

  - Demographic matching: Tests that user profiles with specific age, gender,
    income, and caste criteria correctly match or exclude schemes.

  - Boundary conditions: Validates age range boundaries (e.g., a user aged
    exactly 18 matches a scheme with min_age=18).

  - Multi-criteria matching: Tests scenarios where eligibility depends on
    multiple simultaneous conditions (e.g., female + BPL + student).

  - Empty/null criteria: Ensures schemes with unspecified criteria are not
    incorrectly excluded.


4.2.3  Search Algorithm Tests (test_search.py)

These tests validate the Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF) implementation:

  - Single list ranking: Verifies correct score calculation for results from
    a single search strategy.

  - Multi-list fusion: Tests that schemes appearing in multiple result lists
    receive appropriately boosted scores.

  - Score ordering: Validates that final results are correctly sorted by
    fused RRF scores in descending order.

  - Empty results: Tests behavior when one or more search strategies return
    no results.


4.2.4  API Endpoint Tests (test_schemes_api.py)

These tests verify the REST API endpoints:

  - Response format: Validates JSON response structure against Pydantic schemas.
  - Pagination: Tests page_size and page parameters for correct result counts.
  - Filtering: Validates category, state, level, and keyword filters.
  - Error handling: Tests 404 responses for invalid slugs and 422 for malformed
    requests.


4.2.5  Performance Tests (test_performance.py)

Benchmark tests for critical operations:

  - Search latency: Measures time for keyword, semantic, and hybrid searches
    under various query complexities.
  - Database query performance: Benchmarks common database queries including
    scheme listing, category aggregation, and embedding lookups.
  - Translation throughput: Measures translation service response times.


4.2.6  Search Quality Tests (test_search_quality.py)

These tests evaluate search relevance:

  - Known-good queries: Tests that specific queries return expected schemes
    in the top results (e.g., "PM KISAN" should return the PM-KISAN scheme).
  - Category relevance: Validates that category-specific searches return
    schemes from the correct category.
  - Semantic understanding: Tests that semantically similar but lexically
    different queries return relevant results.


4.2.7  Translation Tests (test_translate_service.py and test_translation_quality.py)

  - Language support: Validates that all 12 supported languages produce
    valid translations.
  - Service fallback: Tests the translation tier fallback chain (Bhashini ->
    IndicTrans -> Google Translate).
  - BLEU score evaluation: Measures translation quality against reference
    translations using the BLEU metric from the sacrebleu library.


4.3  Test Execution
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Tests are executed using pytest from the backend directory:

  pytest tests/                    # Run all tests
  pytest tests/test_chat_service.py # Run specific test module
  pytest --benchmark-only          # Run only benchmark tests

The test suite is designed to be run in both local development and CI/CD
environments. Mock objects are used for external service dependencies (Mistral
API, Bhashini API) to ensure tests run without network access.

6.1  Architecture Evaluation
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Modularity:
  The service-oriented architecture with clear separation between routes,
  services, and models enables independent development and testing of
  components. New features (e.g., a new data source) can be added without
  modifying existing code.

Scalability:
  The async-first approach using FastAPI and asyncpg enables handling
  concurrent requests efficiently. The PostgreSQL database with proper
  indexing supports large datasets. Docker Compose orchestration allows
  horizontal scaling of individual services.

Maintainability:
  TypeScript on the frontend and Pydantic schemas on the backend provide
  type safety. Alembic migrations ensure database schema changes are
  versioned and reversible. The Makefile provides standardized development
  commands.


6.2  Search Quality Evaluation
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The hybrid search approach was evaluated against individual search strategies:

  Keyword-Only:
    Effective for exact matches but fails for semantically related queries.
    Users must know the exact scheme name or terminology.

  Semantic-Only:
    Captures conceptual similarity but may surface loosely related results.
    Works well for descriptive, natural language queries.

  Hybrid (RRF):
    Combines the precision of keyword search with the recall of semantic
    search. Schemes matching both keyword and semantic criteria are ranked
    highest, providing the best user experience.


6.3  Translation Quality Evaluation
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Translation quality was assessed using BLEU scores against reference
translations:

  Bhashini NMT:
    Provides high-quality translations for most Indian language pairs,
    particularly for Hindi, Tamil, and Telugu.

  IndicTrans2:
    The AI4Bharat model shows strong performance for English-to-Indic
    translation, particularly for formal/government language.

  Google Translate Fallback:
    Adequate for general content but may produce less accurate translations
    for domain-specific government terminology.

The multi-tier fallback architecture ensures translation is always available,
with quality degrading gracefully rather than failing completely.


6.4  Technology Stack Analysis
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  FastAPI (Backend):
    Excellent choice for this project due to async support, automatic
    documentation, and high performance. The Pydantic integration provides
    robust input validation with minimal boilerplate.

  Next.js (Frontend):
    The App Router and Server Components enable efficient page rendering.
    The file-based routing convention reduces configuration overhead.
    TypeScript integration provides development-time error detection.

  PostgreSQL + pgvector:
    Consolidating relational data and vector embeddings in a single database
    simplifies the architecture and eliminates the operational overhead of
    managing a separate vector database. Query performance is adequate for
    the current dataset size.

  Mistral AI:
    Provides capable intent classification and response generation at a
    lower cost compared to larger models. The embedding model generates
    high-quality 1024-dimensional vectors suitable for semantic search.

  Docker Compose:
    Enables reproducible multi-service deployment with a single command.
    The GPU passthrough configuration for IndicTrans2 is well-supported.


================================================================================
              CHAPTER 7: CHALLENGES AND LIMITATIONS
================================================================================

7.1  Technical Challenges
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Vector Search Scalability:
    While pgvector performs well for the current dataset of 4,600+ schemes,
    performance may degrade with significantly larger datasets. At scale,
    approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indexes (e.g., IVFFlat or HNSW in
    pgvector) would need to be tuned or a dedicated vector database
    considered.

  LLM Dependency:
    The chatbot's quality is directly tied to the Mistral AI API. Network
    latency, API rate limits, and service outages can impact user experience.
    While a Groq fallback is implemented, it uses a different model family
    which may produce inconsistent responses.

  Translation Accuracy:
    Automated translation of government scheme content, which contains
    legal and technical terminology, poses accuracy challenges. Machine
    translation may not capture the precise meaning of eligibility criteria
    or legal provisions, potentially leading to user confusion.

  Voice Recognition in Noisy Environments:
    The Bhashini ASR service performs well in controlled environments but
    may struggle with background noise, regional accents, and code-mixed
    speech (e.g., Hindi-English mixing), which is common among Indian users.

  Data Freshness:
    Government schemes are frequently updated, modified, or discontinued.
    The current system requires manual re-ingestion to update scheme data.
    There is no automated mechanism for detecting and incorporating scheme
    changes in real-time.


7.2  Limitations
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  No Scheme Application:
    SevanaGPT is limited to scheme discovery and information. It does not
    support actual scheme application or form submission. Users must visit
    official portals to apply for schemes.

  No Identity Verification:
    The eligibility checker relies on self-reported user data. There is no
    integration with Aadhaar, DigiLocker, or other identity verification
    systems to validate user claims.

  Limited Offline Support:
    The application requires an active internet connection for all features.
    There is no offline mode or progressive web app (PWA) functionality,
    which limits access in areas with poor connectivity.

  Single Language per Session:
    While the application supports 12 languages, users must select one
    language for their session. Code-mixed queries (e.g., mixing Hindi and
    English) may not be handled optimally.

  No Personalization Persistence:
    User preferences and eligibility profiles are not persisted across
    sessions. Each visit requires re-entering demographic information for
    eligibility checking.

  No Mobile Native App:
    The current implementation is a responsive web application. A native
    mobile application could provide better voice interaction, push
    notifications for new schemes, and offline caching.

  GPU Requirement for IndicTrans:
    The IndicTrans2 translation microservice requires GPU hardware for
    efficient operation. This increases deployment costs and limits hosting
    options.

8.1  Conclusion
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SevanaGPT successfully demonstrates that AI and modern web technologies can
significantly improve the accessibility of government welfare scheme information
for Indian citizens. The project addresses the critical gap between the
availability of thousands of welfare schemes and citizens' awareness of their
entitlements.

The key accomplishments of this project include:

  1. Comprehensive Scheme Knowledge Base: Successfully aggregated 4,600+
     schemes from five different data sources into a unified, searchable

     recommendations, making the discovery process intuitive.

  6. Production-Ready Architecture: The containerized microservices
